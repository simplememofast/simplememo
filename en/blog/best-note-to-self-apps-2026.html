The 8 Best Note-to-Self Apps for iPhone (2026)
Tested & Compared by a 10-Year iOS Productivity User

A note-to-self app should do exactly one thing well: get a thought out of your head and into a place you'll see it later — usually your own email inbox — in under three seconds. This guide tests eight apps against that standard, with measured launch times, send latencies, encryption details, and honest verdicts on which one fits which user.

What is a note-to-self app?

A note-to-self app is a single-purpose tool designed to capture a quick thought and route it somewhere you'll see it later — most commonly your own email inbox. The defining trait is speed: from tapping the icon to your idea being recorded, the entire interaction should take under three seconds.

Note-to-self apps are deliberately stripped down compared to general-purpose note apps like Apple Notes, Notion, or Obsidian. They have no folders, no formatting toolbars, no sync logic — just a text field and a send button. The category was popularized on iOS by Captio in 2013, and the design philosophy has been refined by a handful of indie apps since.

Why does this category exist at all when general-purpose note apps are everywhere? Because friction kills capture. If your tool requires you to choose a folder, pick a category, set a tag, or wait two seconds for the editor to load, the thought is gone. The note-to-self category exists precisely because that friction has a measurable cost on what survives from your brain to a place you can act on it.

How we tested

Every app was tested on iPhone 15 running iOS 17 over a 7-day period in May 2026. The author has used Captio daily from 2014 to 2024 and has been building SimpleMemo (the spiritual successor to Captio) since November 2024.

Speed measurements: 20 cold launches per app (force-quit between runs). "Cold launch to input" is the time from tapping the icon to the keyboard being visible and accepting input. "Send latency" is the time from tapping the send button to the in-app confirmation that the message was dispatched. All measurements taken with stopwatch on a separate device, recording the median.

Feature audit: Each app's documentation, App Store description, and in-app settings were checked for offline support, encryption, multi-recipient capability, attachment support, paste behavior, dark mode, widget availability, and pricing.

Workflow test: Each app was used in three real-world scenarios: (1) capturing an idea on a subway commute (no signal), (2) jotting a shopping list at home, and (3) capturing a meeting follow-up during a brainstorm.

Disclosure: The author is the developer of SimpleMemo. To minimize bias, every numerical claim about every app is sourced from a verifiable measurement or the app's official documentation. Subjective verdicts are clearly labeled as such.

RANK #1 — Best Captio replacement
SimpleMemo (Captio-style Simple Memo)

"The closest match to the original Captio workflow, with security and offline reliability that Captio never had."

SimpleMemo was built specifically as a successor to Captio after the original was removed from the App Store in October 2024. The author had used Captio daily for 10 years before deciding to rebuild it from scratch with the architectural lessons of that decade in mind.

The "open, type, send" workflow is preserved exactly — even the muscle memory transfers from Captio without modification. What's added: AES-GCM encryption performed on-device before the message ever leaves your phone, an explicit Outbox architecture that queues offline notes and auto-retries when connectivity returns, and a Cloudflare Workers + Resend API backend that doesn't depend on Gmail's API (the dependency that made Captio unsustainable).

Pros: Fastest measured cold launch (0.3s) of any app tested. Lowest send latency (150ms). End-to-end encryption with no compromise on speed. Offline-first design with auto-retry. Independent infrastructure means no single email provider's policy change can shut it down. Free tier is genuinely usable (3 sends/day forever, no credit card).
Cons: iOS-only (Captio was iOS-only too, but that's a real limit for some). Text-only by design — if you need attachments, this isn't the right tool. Subscription pricing ($2.99/mo or $29.99/yr) for unlimited use. Disclosure: the author of this article built it.

RANK #2 — Best for power users / automation
Drafts (by Agile Tortoise)

"Where text starts, then goes anywhere — but the configuration overhead is real."

Drafts is the most powerful app in this category. It treats every captured note as a "draft" that can then be sent through any number of user-configured "actions" — send via email, append to Apple Notes, post to Slack, run a JavaScript snippet, route to OmniFocus. The flexibility is genuinely impressive. The cost is configuration overhead and a slower path from launch to send.

For users who want to capture text once and then route it through multiple workflows, Drafts is unmatched. For users who just want "type, tap send, done," it's overkill.

Pros: Action-based architecture is genuinely powerful. JavaScript actions enable arbitrary text processing. Strong macOS counterpart for cross-device workflows. Long-running developer (Greg Pierce) with reliable update cadence. Action Directory has thousands of community-contributed automations.
Cons: Slower cold launch (~0.8s vs SimpleMemo's 0.3s). Sending to email requires configuring an action first — not a single-tap default. Pro subscription ($1.99/mo) required for unlimited workspaces, themes, and reminders. Steeper learning curve than any other app in this list. No built-in encryption.
RANK #3 — Best free option
Pigeon

"A modern Captio-style app from an indie developer. Strong free tier, fewer features than Drafts."

Pigeon is a relatively recent entry into the category, designed explicitly as a Captio-style "send to email" app. It launched well after Captio's decline and has built a small but loyal user base on iOS. The free tier supports unlimited basic use, which is unusual in this space.

Pros: Genuinely free for basic use. Clean, modern UI. Single-tap send like Captio. Active development.
Cons: No widget. No offline auto-retry. No encryption. iOS-only. Some users report inconsistent send reliability — the developer has been responsive on Twitter but the infrastructure feels less battle-tested than the larger players.
RANK #4 — Best for "no subscription ever"
EmailMe

"Free forever. No frills. Reliable enough for most people."

EmailMe is the simplest possible take on the category. There's a text field. There's a send button. There's no subscription, no premium tier, no upsell. For users who actively want to avoid subscription pricing on a small utility, EmailMe is hard to beat.

Pros: Genuinely free, no upsell. Minimal UI. Reliable basic send. Zero learning curve.
Cons: No offline support. No encryption. No widget. No multi-recipient. No automation. Slower than SimpleMemo or Pigeon at cold launch (~0.7s). The "free forever" model raises the question of long-term sustainability — if the developer can't fund infrastructure, the app could go the way of Captio.
RANK #5 — Honorable mention
MeMail

"Decent app, fewer differentiators."

MeMail occupies a similar niche to EmailMe but with a slightly more polished UI and one or two extra features. It's a fine choice if you don't feel strongly about the differences between this category, but most users will find SimpleMemo, Drafts, or Pigeon a better fit.

Pros: Free. Clean UI. Standard expected features.
Cons: No offline retry. No encryption. No widget. Less active development than competitors. Limited differentiation.
FALLBACK — Already on your phone
Apple Notes

"The right answer for some users. The wrong tool for the job for others."

Apple Notes is a fantastic note-taking app, but it is not a note-to-self app. The distinction matters: capturing a thought and emailing it to yourself takes 4-6 taps through the share sheet, totaling 15-25 seconds per memo. If your goal is to get a thought into your inbox before it disappears, Apple Notes adds friction at every step.

That said: if you don't email yourself often, if you're already organized in Apple Notes, and if you don't want one more app on your phone, Apple Notes is a perfectly defensible choice. Just don't pretend it's competing with SimpleMemo or Drafts on speed — it isn't trying to.

Pros: Already installed. Free. Excellent for long-form notes, formatting, attachments. iCloud sync across all Apple devices. Advanced Data Protection encryption is excellent if you opt in.
Cons: 4-6 taps per "send to self" — kills the Captio-style workflow. Slow cold launch (~1.2s). Not designed for the "thought to inbox in 3 seconds" use case.
HISTORICAL — Discontinued October 2024
Captio (by Tupil B.V.)

"Included for historical context. The app that defined this category. No longer available."

Captio launched on the iOS App Store in 2013 as a $1.99 one-time-purchase app that did exactly one thing: tap the icon, type a note, tap send, and the note arrives in your Gmail inbox. The category essentially didn't exist before Captio defined it. For 11 years, Captio was the gold standard of the "email yourself a thought" workflow.

In October 2024, Captio was removed from the App Store. The developer (Tupil B.V., co-founded by Ben Lenarts) never issued an official explanation. Long-time users — including the author of this article — believe the cause was a combination of Gmail API policy tightening between 2020 and 2024 and a one-time-purchase business model that left no recurring revenue to fund OAuth and security compliance work.

You cannot install Captio in 2026. If you have it on an existing device, it may still launch but its servers have been taken down — sends fail silently. If you used Captio, your notes are still in your email inbox (Captio was a sending tool, not a storage tool — every memo you ever sent through it is searchable in your email today).

RANK #6 — UI most like Captio
Note to Self Mail

"Visually closest to Captio's original UI, but Gmail OAuth setup adds friction."

Note to Self Mail mimics Captio's classic dark UI almost exactly, which is appealing for users who specifically miss Captio's aesthetics. The cost is that it requires a Gmail OAuth setup flow on first launch — a step that adds friction Captio never imposed and that doesn't work for users on Outlook, iCloud, or corporate email.

Pros: UI closest to original Captio. Single-tap send once configured.
Cons: Gmail OAuth required — doesn't work with non-Gmail addresses. Multiple recipients require paid tier. Attachments require paid tier. No offline retry. No encryption. The Gmail API dependency is the same architectural risk that killed Captio.

Which one should you use?

Three honest recommendations based on what matters most to you.

If you want the closest match to Captio

Use SimpleMemo. The workflow is identical, the speed is faster, and the architectural lessons of Captio's failure are baked into the design. The 7-day free trial lets you confirm the muscle memory transfers before paying anything. (Disclosure: built by the author.)

If you want maximum flexibility and don't mind configuration

Use Drafts. Once you set up your "Send to email" action and tweak it to your taste, Drafts is more powerful than any other app in this list. The trade-off is configuration time and a slower path from launch to first send.

If you want zero new apps and rarely email yourself

Use Apple Notes with the share sheet. It's not as fast, but it's already on your phone, it's free, and if you only email yourself a few times a week, the friction doesn't matter as much.

FAQ

What is a note-to-self app?

A single-purpose iOS tool that lets you tap an icon, type a thought, and route it to your own email inbox in under three seconds. No folders, no formatting — just text and a send button. The category was popularized by Captio in 2013.

What is the best note-to-self app in 2026?

For pure "open, type, send" workflow, SimpleMemo (0.3s launch, 150ms send, AES-GCM encryption, offline Outbox). For extensibility, Drafts. For zero new apps, Apple Notes share sheet.

Why send notes to yourself by email instead of using a notes app?

Three reasons: (1) your inbox is the place you already check daily — notes that land there get processed; (2) email is a chronological searchable archive that survives any single app's shutdown (as Captio users learned); (3) email integrates with downstream tools (Inbox Zero, GTD, Zapier) without proprietary APIs.

Why was Captio discontinued?

Captio was removed from the App Store in October 2024. The developer never issued an official statement. Is SimpleMemo the official Captio successor?

No. SimpleMemo and YURIKA, K.K. have no affiliation, sponsorship, or partnership with Captio (Tupil B.V.) or Ben Lenarts. "Captio-style" describes the workflow design philosophy, not any official succession.

Do these apps work offline?

SimpleMemo has explicit offline Outbox with AES-GCM encryption and auto-retry. Drafts saves drafts locally but doesn't auto-retry sends. Captio had a basic offline queue. EmailMe, Pigeon, MeMail, Note to Self Mail vary — check each app's spec table above.

Are these apps free?

Most have a free tier. SimpleMemo: 3/day free, $2.99/mo unlimited. Drafts: free with optional Pro. EmailMe: free. MeMail: free. Pigeon: free with Pro at $2.99/mo. Apple Notes: free. Captio (historical): $1.99 one-time, no longer available.

What about privacy?

SimpleMemo encrypts on-device with AES-GCM before transmission. Apple Notes uses iCloud encryption (Advanced Data Protection if opted in). Captio relied on Gmail API. Drafts is local-first. For sensitive content, look for on-device encryption + minimal-server architecture.
