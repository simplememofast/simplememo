What Happened to Captio? (A Brief Timeline)

If you are reading this, you probably already know: Captio, the beloved "one-tap email to self" app for iOS, is gone. But the story of how it disappeared is worth understanding, because it reveals a structural vulnerability that affected not just Captio but dozens of productivity apps that relied on Gmail integration.

Captio launched around 2010 and quickly became a cult favorite among productivity enthusiasts, GTD practitioners, and anyone who needed to capture quick thoughts without friction. The concept was brilliantly simple: open the app, type your note, tap send. The memo arrives in your email inbox instantly. No folders, no categories, no sync conflicts. Just a direct pipeline from your brain to your inbox.

For years, Captio worked flawlessly because it used the Gmail API to deliver messages directly to your inbox. This was the technical foundation of the entire product. Gmail's API allowed Captio to bypass traditional SMTP delivery, which meant near-instant delivery times and reliable placement in your primary inbox rather than spam.

The trouble started in 2023 when Google began tightening its OAuth scope restrictions. Google's "Restricted Scope" policy required apps accessing sensitive Gmail data to undergo rigorous security assessments, including third-party audits costing tens of thousands of dollars annually. For a small development team maintaining an app with a one-time purchase price of a few dollars, the math simply did not work out.

By mid-2024, the writing was on the wall. Google's API policy changes had made it impractical for small developers to maintain Gmail API access. In October 2024, Captio was officially removed from the App Store, and the sending infrastructure went dark. Users who still had Captio installed found that the send button no longer worked.

The silver lining: your old Captio data is safe. Because Captio sent everything as emails, every memo you ever wrote is sitting in your inbox (or wherever you archived it). There is nothing to export or migrate -- your data already lives in your email.

But the loss of Captio left a real gap. No other app at the time offered the same combination of instant launch, one-tap send, and zero-clutter interface. That is what prompted our search for the best alternatives -- and this hands-on comparison.

How We Tested: Methodology

Speed claims are everywhere in the App Store. Every note-taking app promises to be "fast" or "instant." We wanted actual numbers, so we designed a reproducible testing methodology that measures real-world performance down to the millisecond.

Here is exactly how we conducted our tests:

  • Test device: iPhone 15 Pro running iOS 17.4. The device was restarted before each test session to ensure a clean state. No other apps were running in the background.
  • Network conditions: All tests were performed twice -- once on Wi-Fi (100Mbps symmetric fiber connection) and once on cellular (5G, with signal strength of 3-4 bars). The speed figures reported are Wi-Fi results unless otherwise noted.
  • Recording method: We used an external camera recording at 240fps pointed at the iPhone screen. This allowed us to count exact frames between the tap event and the app's ready-to-type state (launch speed) and between the send-button tap and the confirmation animation (send speed).
  • Sample size: Each app was tested three times for each metric. We report the median value to eliminate outliers caused by background system processes.
  • Cold start vs. warm start: Launch speed measurements use cold starts (app not in memory). This represents the real-world scenario of grabbing your phone to capture a quick thought.
  • What we measured:
    • Launch-to-ready time: From the moment the app icon is tapped to the moment the text cursor is blinking and the keyboard is visible.
    • Tap-to-sent time: From the moment the send button is tapped to the moment the app displays a sent confirmation (animation, checkmark, or screen clear).

We also evaluated each app on qualitative criteria: offline capability, encryption and privacy, pricing structure, and how closely the workflow matches Captio's original experience. For a deeper dive into our benchmarking methodology, see our full speed benchmark article.

App-by-App Deep Dive

1. Simple Memo -- The Purpose-Built Captio Replacement

Simple Memo was designed from the ground up to solve the exact problem Captio's shutdown created. The workflow is intentionally identical: open the app, type your note, tap send. The memo lands in your email inbox within seconds. The screen clears automatically, ready for your next thought. There is no note organization, no folder management, no sync -- just a fast pipeline from brain to inbox.

What sets Simple Memo apart technically is its independent infrastructure. Instead of relying on Gmail's API (which killed Captio), Simple Memo uses Cloudflare Workers for the backend and Resend API for email delivery. This means it works with any email provider -- Gmail, Outlook, Yahoo Mail, iCloud, corporate Exchange, or anything else. There is no single point of failure tied to one company's API policies.

The offline capability is something Captio never had. When you tap send without a network connection, Simple Memo encrypts your memo with AES-GCM and stores it in a local Outbox. When connectivity returns, the app silently sends all queued messages in order. You never lose a thought because you were in the subway or on a plane.

The encryption deserves a mention: every memo is encrypted on-device before transmission using AES-GCM. The plaintext never touches Simple Memo's servers. For anyone sending sensitive notes -- meeting summaries, personal reminders, passwords, medical information -- this is a meaningful privacy advantage over every other option on this list.

  • Fastest launch (0.3s) and send (~150ms) of any tested app
  • Offline Outbox with automatic retry
  • AES-GCM encryption on-device
  • Works with any email provider
  • Identical workflow to Captio
  • Send history viewable in-app
  • iOS only (no Android version yet)
  • Free plan limited to 3 sends per day
  • No rich text formatting (plain text only)
Best for: Former Captio users, speed-focused note-takers, privacy-conscious users
Pricing: Free (3 sends/day) / Premium $2.99/month or $29.99/year (unlimited sends, 7-day free trial)

2. Drafts -- The Power User's Text Processor

Drafts is an excellent app, but it is fundamentally a different product than Captio. Where Captio and Simple Memo are single-purpose tools (capture a thought, send it to email), Drafts is a full-featured text processing hub. You type text, and then you decide what to do with it: send to email, save to Files, post to Twitter, append to a note in Bear, or trigger any of hundreds of custom actions.

The launch speed is respectable at 0.6-0.7 seconds, and Drafts immediately opens to a blank note with the cursor active -- a thoughtful design choice that Captio also used. However, sending to email requires tapping through an action (or configuring a custom action), which adds friction and pushes the total send time to around 500ms after configuration. Out of the box, it takes several more taps.

Drafts stores everything locally first, so you can write offline. But the email-send action requires a network connection -- there is no offline queue that automatically retries like Simple Memo's Outbox. If you tap "send to email" while offline, the action fails and you must manually retry later.

The free version of Drafts is surprisingly capable, but the email actions and advanced automation require the Pro subscription at $4.99 per month. For users who want Captio's simplicity, Drafts may feel over-engineered. But for power users who want a capture tool that can route text to dozens of destinations, Drafts is unmatched.

  • Opens directly to a blank note
  • Extremely flexible action system
  • Can send to email, messaging apps, task managers, and more
  • Robust Apple Watch app
  • Strong community with shared actions
  • Email send requires extra taps (not one-tap)
  • No offline email queue
  • No encryption for stored drafts
  • Pro subscription ($4.99/mo) needed for email actions
  • Steeper learning curve
Best for: Power users who want text routing to multiple destinations, not just email
Pricing: Free (basic) / Pro $4.99/month or $29.99/year

3. Apple Notes + Share Sheet -- The "Already on Your Phone" Option

You do not need to install anything for this approach. Open Apple Notes, type your thought, tap the share button, and choose Mail. It works, and for occasional use, it is fine. But the workflow has significant friction compared to a purpose-built tool like Captio.

The launch speed of Apple Notes itself is quite good at 0.4-0.5 seconds. But the total time to send a note to your email is much longer. After typing, you tap Share, wait for the Share Sheet to animate in (~0.5s), scroll to find Mail, tap it, wait for the mail composer to load (~0.8s), then tap Send. The entire sequence from "I have a thought" to "it is in my inbox" takes roughly 5-8 seconds with at least four taps. Compare that to Simple Memo's two taps and 0.45 seconds total.

On the positive side, Apple Notes syncs via iCloud, so your notes are backed up and accessible from any Apple device. If you do not actually need the note in your email inbox and just want to capture text quickly, Apple Notes alone (without the email step) is a solid option. But if your workflow requires email delivery -- which is the entire point of a Captio alternative -- the Share Sheet adds too much friction for frequent use.

There is no encryption beyond what iCloud provides, and there is no offline-to-email queue. If you try to email a note while offline, the mail will sit in your Mail app's outbox (not in Notes), and you need the Mail app configured separately.

  • Already installed on every iPhone
  • No cost whatsoever
  • iCloud sync across Apple devices
  • Rich text, images, and attachments supported
  • 4+ taps to email a note (not one-tap)
  • ~5-8 seconds total workflow time
  • Share Sheet adds friction every single time
  • No dedicated "send to email" shortcut
  • Notes accumulate and require manual cleanup
Best for: Users who rarely email notes to themselves and want a zero-cost, zero-install option
Pricing: Free (built into iOS)

4. Note to Self Mail -- The Closest Concept, Slower Execution

Note to Self Mail is a third-party app that attempts to replicate the Captio concept: type a note and send it to your own email address. The intent is right, and the app delivers on the basic promise. But the execution is noticeably slower, and it lacks critical features that made Captio reliable.

Launch speed averages around 1.0 second -- roughly three times slower than Simple Memo. The interface loads, but there is a visible delay before the keyboard appears and the cursor is ready. Send speed is approximately 800ms, which is acceptable but not instant. The total workflow from launch to sent confirmation is roughly 1.8 seconds, compared to Simple Memo's 0.45 seconds.

The bigger concern is reliability. Note to Self Mail has no offline support: if you are without a network connection, the send fails and there is no automatic retry. There is also no encryption -- your memo text is transmitted in the clear. For quick grocery lists, this probably does not matter. For anything sensitive, it is a problem.

The app is free with no premium tier, which is appealing if budget is your primary concern. But the lack of offline support and encryption, combined with the slower speed, means it does not fully replicate the reliability that Captio users came to depend on.

  • Completely free, no subscription
  • Simple interface focused on email-to-self
  • No account creation required
  • Slow launch (~1.0s) and send (~800ms)
  • No offline support -- sends fail without network
  • No encryption
  • No send history
  • Less polished UI
Best for: Users who want a free, no-frills email-to-self app and do not need speed or offline support
Pricing: Free

5. iPhone Shortcuts -- The DIY Approach

Apple's Shortcuts app lets you build a custom automation that replicates Captio's workflow: show a text input dialog, take the entered text, and send it as an email to your own address. It is a clever solution that costs nothing and requires no third-party apps. But in practice, it is the slowest option we tested by a significant margin.

Launching a Shortcut from the home screen takes approximately 1.5 seconds. The Shortcuts runtime needs to initialize, parse the automation, and render the input dialog. Then, after you type and confirm, the email-send action takes roughly 3 seconds because it invokes the system Mail framework in the background, which includes authentication and SMTP handshake overhead for each send.

The total workflow time from tap to sent is roughly 4.5 seconds plus typing time. For comparison, Simple Memo completes the entire sequence in under half a second plus typing time -- nearly ten times faster. Over the course of a day with 10-20 quick captures, that difference adds up to minutes of friction.

Shortcuts also has no offline capability for email actions. If you run the shortcut without a network connection, the email step fails and the entire automation errors out. There is no queue, no retry, and no local storage of the failed memo. Your thought is simply lost unless you manually copy it elsewhere before dismissing the error.

That said, Shortcuts has one unique advantage: infinite customizability. You can add timestamps, location data, tags, or route memos to different email addresses based on conditions. If you enjoy building automations and want a highly personalized workflow, Shortcuts is the only option that gives you full control over every step.

  • Completely free, built into iOS
  • Fully customizable workflow
  • Can add timestamps, location, tags automatically
  • No third-party app required
  • Slowest option tested (~4.5s total)
  • No offline support -- fails without network
  • No encryption
  • Requires technical setup knowledge
  • Fragile -- iOS updates can break automations
  • No send history
Best for: Tinkerers who enjoy building automations and want maximum customization
Pricing: Free (built into iOS)
